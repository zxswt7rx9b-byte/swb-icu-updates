Clinical Updates

Critical Care Extubation Checklist — Now Live

A new critical care extubation checklist is now available in clinical practice. Please take a moment to familiarise yourself with it.

About the checklist It supports a structured and consistent assessment of readiness for extubation while maintaining clinical judgement, in line with Difficult Airway Society (DAS) principles. The aim is to enhance patient safety and consistency across the unit.

How to use it:

  • Before ward rounds — use the A-E framework in the first column to guide your readiness assessment
  • At the time of extubation — work through columns two onwards
  • Document extubation assessments and checklist use in the clinical notes
  • Laminated copies are in the document drawers/trays at each bedside
  • An electronic version will be available on SPARC shortly
  • A dedicated UNITY documentation template is in development

Fistula — Laminated Bedspace Sheets

Laminated sheets are now available to display at the bedspace when your patient has a fistula. Please use these to support clear communication and safe care.

Operational Reminders
  • Slide sheets on transfers: When transferring a patient to CT scan or the ward, please ensure the patient's slide sheets travel with them.
  • Medications on ward transfers: When a patient is transferred to the ward, please ensure their medications go with them to avoid any delay in their care on arrival.
  • Cleaning checklist — staff responsibility: Domestics do not clean medical equipment (Gratnell trolleys, monitors, ventilators, beds) when carrying out a barrier clean. Staff must complete a cleaning checklist after all discharges and bed moves.
  • Calreacia / Cifoban packaging: Bags returned to the storeroom out of their original packaging require disposal at £50 per bag. Please ensure bags are returned in their original packaging.
  • Baton phones — primary communication: Baton phones are the primary means of communication on shift. Keep them charged, ringers on, and carry them throughout your shift. One for the nurse in charge, one for the ICU registrar, two SHO phones (one per zone), two ACCP phones (one per zone). Please avoid using personal mobiles when contacting the on-call consultant — unknown numbers may be screened out in an emergency.

Policy Updates

RAAC Support in ICU

RAAC Framework — Key Points RAAC support is intended to be short-duration and clearly defined (e.g. line insertion, short-term procedural support), with early consultant involvement if ongoing input is needed.

If a task is likely to be prolonged (roughly more than one hour), the RAAC doctor should discuss with the ICU on-call consultant, who will carry out a dynamic risk assessment and decide on resource allocation — including planning for what happens if the RAAC doctor is needed for emergency duties elsewhere.

There is currently no mandatory maximum duration — this is an advisory framework, dependent on dynamic risk assessment.

Zone 2 Rest Areas — Night Shifts

No area within Zone 2 may be used as a rest area (including for sleeping) during night shifts, other than Room 17.

Evening Safety Brief

The ICU registrar-tier doctor is expected to attend the evening safety brief to contribute to planning for the night shift.

Uniform & Appearance

Above all else, we value the clinical excellence and teamwork you bring every day. That said, we do ask all staff to meet Trust expectations around professional presentation.

  • ICU uniforms only when on duty — no theatre scrubs
  • Yellow name badge visible at all times
  • No lanyards
  • Hand hygiene before and after every patient contact — bare below the elbows in all clinical areas
  • No long, synthetic, or varnished nails
  • Hair collar length or above
  • Jewellery to a minimum — one plain ring, one pair of plain studs if earrings worn
  • Cardigans and jackets removed in clinical areas

Thank you for everything you do — the positives are very much noticed.
